About

After five years in Paris working in audiovisual, video and film production, Louis Guichard left for New York. He spent two years there, criss-crossing the United States — camera in hand, following the road from one coast to the next, drawn to the light of places he had never seen and to the people he met along the way.

He does not think of photography as the making of images. An image shows; a photograph reveals.

For him, a photograph is less a proof of the real than a trace of our passage through it. Each one is a quiet way of saying: I was here, this is what I saw, this is what stopped me.

He does not seek to document the world with precision. What interests him is the fragile space between what exists and what we project onto things. Photography, to him, is a deeply subjective language — two people can look at the same scene and never see the same image.

To photograph is to inscribe one's gaze into the matter of the real: to turn an intimate experience into an invitation addressed to a stranger. A form of trust. A silent promise made to the viewer — that they may inhabit the image with their own story.

In a time when everything must be immediate, explained and consumed at once, he likes the idea that a photograph can still resist — that it can leave room for mystery, for interpretation, for what goes unsaid.

Because what fascinates him is not what the image shows, but what it sets in motion.

A photograph is not an answer. It is an encounter between a gaze, a memory and an imagination.
